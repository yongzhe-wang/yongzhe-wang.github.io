Winning the Anthropic × Penn Hackathon with Vertices

April 2026

Last weekend, four of us stayed up for 36 hours straight in a windowless room on Penn's campus and built an AI broker for people. We called it Vertices, and it won 1st place overall at the Anthropic × Penn AI Hackathon. This is the short story of why we built it and what we learned.

The problem

Everyone in tech has had this moment: you're trying to connect with someone, and the only surface available is a polished bio — a LinkedIn headline, a carefully written Twitter pinned tweet, a founder page. None of it tells you what the person is actually working on this week. None of it tells you what they're stuck on. None of it tells you if there's a real reason the two of you should talk.

We wanted to skip the bio layer entirely. What if the input to a people-matching system wasn't your marketing copy, but the raw context you were already producing — your notes, your half-written docs, your Slack rants, your browser history, the messy text files on your desktop?

The insight

Polished surfaces are low-signal. Unguarded context is high-signal. If you let someone dump everything — and I mean everything — and let a model sit with it, the model can do something a recruiter or a cold-DM can't: identify what you're truly spending your energy on right now, and find the one other person in the room who would make your week better.

That was the whole bet. Real signal over polished surface. Raw working material over curated narrative.

How we built it

We built Vertices on top of the Ara track. Ara (YC P26) is building the personal AI layer — the place where your unstructured context lives — so it was the natural substrate. Our job was the agent on top.

The pipeline was simple and aggressively unglamorous:

First, ingestion. Upload anything — PDFs, markdown, chat logs, screenshots of whiteboards. We didn't try to be clever about formats; we just normalized everything into a big bag of text chunks.

Second, a synthesis agent. This is where Claude did the heavy lifting. Instead of embedding-based similarity, we asked the model to write, in its own words, a few paragraphs describing what the person is actually working on, what they're stuck on, and what kind of conversation would move them forward. That paragraph became the person's "working state."

Third, a matching agent. Given two working states, does a real introduction make sense? Why? The output wasn't a cosine score — it was an argument, in English, that either made the intro worth making or didn't.

Four people. Thirty-six hours. One very loud AirPod war over which model to use for the matcher.

What we learned

The biggest lesson was about input quality. The demos that landed hardest were the ones where the user had dumped the messiest material — unfinished thoughts, contradictions, half-formed ideas. That's where the matches surprised people. When the input was already a tidy bio, the output was also already tidy, and there was no magic.

The second lesson: matching is a writing task, not a retrieval task. Framing every step as "have the model write an argument" — rather than "compute a score" — made the system feel less like a recommender and more like a thoughtful friend who happens to know everyone at the party.

Thanks

Huge thanks to Ara (YC P26), Anthropic, and the Claude Builder Club @ Penn for putting this together — real stakes, real infra, real Claude quota. And to my teammates Andy Wang, Winson Wang, and Hugo Song: I would not trade those 36 hours for any amount of sleep.
